Vortex-induced vibration is a resonance problem: a cylindrical member sheds vortices
at a frequency set by flow speed, and if that frequency approaches a structural mode
the member is driven at resonance. The engineering question is not "what is the
shedding frequency" but "how much margin do I have".

This example answers the second question, which means the flow solve is a step in
the middle rather than the deliverable.

## What runs per row

Each airspeed gets a full DEC incompressible Navier–Stokes march past a circular
member. A probe in the wake records transverse velocity; the shedding frequency
comes from that signal, and the Strouhal number follows as `St = f·D/U`.

Four marches run concurrently under the `parallel` feature. The example notes that
the concurrent result is bit-identical to the sequential one — worth stating, because
a wake solve that changed answers under threading would invalidate the whole table.

## The output

```text
airspeed [m/s]   Re      St      f_shed [Hz]   margin
      0.75       100  0.1818        68.2     0.545
      0.90       120  0.1818        81.8     0.455
      1.05       140  0.1909       100.2     0.332
      1.20       160  0.1909       114.5     0.236
```

Member `D = 2.0 mm`, structural mode `f_struct = 150 Hz`.

The margin column is what the run exists to produce, and it falls monotonically:
0.545 → 0.236. Extrapolate one more step and you cross the 0.15 placard. The
table's job is to show where the envelope ends, which it does without ever
reaching a failure.

## The gates

```text
[PASS] strouhal band: extracted St in [0.1818, 0.1909], validated band [0.16, 0.21] for this grid
[PASS] resonance margin: min |f_struct - f_shed| / f_struct = 0.236, placard minimum 0.15
[PASS] finite wake: 4 sweeps returned a finite, oscillating wake
```

The first gate is the interesting one. It does not check St against the textbook
0.164–0.21 range for a smooth cylinder; it checks against a band **validated for
this grid**. That distinction is deliberate. A coarse grid biases Strouhal high,
which is visible here — the extracted values sit at the upper end. Gating against
the literature value would either fail a correct run or force a grid nobody can
afford in a sweep.

The third gate looks trivial and is not. A wake march that decays to a steady
solution produces a *finite* signal with no oscillation, and the frequency
extractor will happily report noise as a frequency. See the
[cylinder verification](/validation/#dec-cylinder), where a staircase boundary
does exactly that and reports a Strouhal of 0.244 from seventh-decimal noise.

## Where this stops being enough

The sweep covers Re 100 to 160. That is laminar shedding, and nothing here claims
otherwise. A flight-Reynolds strut sits orders of magnitude above this range and
waits on the [staged turbulence work](/boundaries/#turbulence).

The structural frequency and the 0.15 placard are stated demonstration values, not
measured properties of a real member. Substitute your own before reading anything
into the margin column.
