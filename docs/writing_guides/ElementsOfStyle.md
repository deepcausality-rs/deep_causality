# Elements of Style: A Practical Writing Guideline

Distilled from four major traditions in English prose writing: Strunk & White (*The Elements of Style*), George Orwell (*Politics and the English Language*), William Zinsser (*On Writing Well*), and Steven Pinker (*The Sense of Style*). The four disagree on details. They agree on what follows.

## The core principle

Writing is one mind helping another mind see something. Every rule, every mechanic, every style choice exists to serve that. If the reader cannot follow you, nothing else you did counts.

## Five operational principles

### 1. Clarity is the only test that matters

Pinker calls the cause of unclear writing *the curse of knowledge*: the writer fails to imagine that the reader does not already know what the writer knows. Zinsser's version of the same point: *"If the reader is lost, it is usually because the writer hasn't been careful enough."*

The antidote is operational: Assume the reader knows less than you do. Show a draft to a representative reader and watch where they stumble. 

### 2. Cut what does not pull its weight

Three traditions converge here. Strunk: *"Omit needless words."* Orwell: *"If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out."* Zinsser: *"Strip every sentence to its cleanest components."*

Specific things to cut on sight:

- **Hedges** that add nothing but apology: *somewhat, comparatively, to a certain degree, perhaps, arguably*.
- **Throat-clearing openers**: *It is important to note that*, *In a sense*, *There is a tendency to*, *It should be mentioned that*.
- **Redundant qualifiers**: *true facts*, *advance planning*, *end result*, *completely eliminate*, *very unique*, *first and foremost*.
- **Adverbs that amplify what the verb already says**: *really*, *very*, *basically*, *actually*, *literally* (when not literal).
- **Wind-up phrases** before the real point: *what I want to say is*, *the point I want to make is*.

### 3. Prefer concrete nouns and active verbs

Strunk: *"Write with nouns and verbs."* Orwell: *"Never use a long word where a short one will do. Never use the passive where you can use the active."* Pinker: replace *zombie nouns* (often ending in `-tion`, `-ization`, `-ment`) with the verbs they hide.

| Hidden verb | Active rewrite |
|---|---|
| *Implementation of the policy was carried out* | *we implemented the policy* / *we did X* |
| *Made the decision to* | *decided to* |
| *Utilize* | *use* (unless *utilize* carries a shade *use* cannot) |
| *Provides an explanation of* | *explains* |
| *In the event that* | *if* |

The active verb names the actor. The passive hides it. Sometimes hiding is correct (the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or institutionally diffuse). Most of the time, hiding is laziness.

### 4. Put the emphatic word at the end

Strunk's emphatic-position rule and Pinker's *old-before-new* rule say the same thing. The start of a sentence is where the reader expects what they already know (anchor). The end is where their attention concentrates (payoff). Put the light familiar material first; put the new heavy material last.

- Weak: *"There is a difference of forty units between Y(1) and Y(0)."*
- Strong: *"Y(1) minus Y(0) is forty units."*
- Weak: *"It was Pearl who introduced the abduction step in counterfactual inference."*
- Strong: *"Pearl introduced the abduction step in counterfactual inference."*

### 5. Connect

Each sentence should follow visibly from the one before it. Each paragraph should follow from the one before. Pinker calls this *coherence*. If a reader has to backtrack to figure out how they got here, you broke it.

Concrete techniques:

- Repeat key nouns when synonyms would confuse the reference.
- Use connective words sparingly and precisely. *Additionally*, *furthermore*, *moreover* at the start of paragraphs is a tell that the writer skipped the connection step.
- Old information at the start of a sentence anchors; new at the end lands. Across sentences, the last noun of one becomes the first noun of the next ("noun chaining").

## Two stance principles

### 6. Write as one person speaking to another

Zinsser: *"There is no style store; style is organic to the person doing the writing."*

Pinker's *classic style*: a writer who has seen something points it out to a reader who is capable of seeing it too. The right person is on the page; the reader is the right one too. Institutional voice (*it has been determined that*, *the present author*) erases the person, then everyone else, then the meaning.

### 7. The real work is in the revision

Zinsser: *"There is no good writing, only good rewriting."* The drafts split roughly as follows.

- **First draft** puts something on the page.
- **Second draft** cuts.
- **Third draft** restructures.
- **Fourth draft** replaces the wrong word.
- **Fifth draft** kills the favourite sentence that does not earn its place.

A draft that has only been written once has not been written.

## Mechanics

These are diagnostic. When a draft violates them, it is usually violating one of the principles above. Fixing the deeper principle fixes the symptom.

### Sentence-length variation

A page of fifteen-word sentences reads like a metronome. Mix short with occasional long. A four-word sentence after a thirty-word one earns emphasis for free.

| Sentence-length pattern | Reads as |
|---|---|
| 14, 16, 15, 17, 14 | Mechanical, AI-flavoured. |
| 8, 23, 4, 19, 12, 31 | Natural human rhythm. |

### Punctuation

- **Em-dashes** are fine in moderation. Reaching for them as the default conjunction signals rushed prose. Aim for no more than one per 250 words on average; the AI-text average is one per 75 to 125 words.
- **Semicolons** exist for a reason. Use them where a period would over-break and a comma would under-break. Their near-absence in a long-form piece is its own tell.
- **Commas** should serve breath and meaning, not decoration. *"He walked to the store, and bought milk"* has a wrong comma. Either drop it, or break into two sentences.

### Word choice

- Short words beat long words unless the long word is honest. *Use* beats *utilize*. *About* beats *regarding*. *Now* beats *at the present time*. *Help* beats *facilitate*.
- A common concept (such as *important*) does not need rotated synonyms (*crucial*, *vital*, *essential*, *significant*) on every appearance. Pick the right word and use it. The rotation is a tell.

### Banned phrases

Stock AI clichés that should not appear in serious prose:

- *delve into*
- *shed light on*
- *game-changer*
- *unlock the potential*
- *not only X but also Y*

### Filler words

*Very* and *really* appear two to three times more often in machine-written prose than in human-written prose. Each instance is an opportunity to either delete the word or pick a stronger verb. *"Very tired"* is usually *exhausted*, *wiped*, or *shattered*.

### Read it aloud

The single test that catches more problems than any rule. Sentences that work read smoothly. Sentences that do not work catch the tongue.

## A six-pass revision checklist

1. **Cut pass.** What can come out?
2. **Verb pass.** Is every sentence's main verb concrete and active?
3. **Order pass.** Old before new; light before heavy; emphatic word at the end?
4. **Cohesion pass.** Does each sentence visibly follow from the previous?
5. **Voice pass.** Could a stranger reading this feel a person behind it?
6. **Reader pass.** Show it to one representative reader. Where they stumble, fix the prose; do not blame the reader.

## What the four traditions agree on, in one line each

- **Strunk & White** (*The Elements of Style*): omit needless words; write with nouns and verbs.
- **Orwell** (*Politics and the English Language*): never use a long word where a short one will do, and *break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous*.
- **Zinsser** (*On Writing Well*): clear thinking becomes clear writing; one cannot exist without the other.
- **Pinker** (*The Sense of Style*): the writer's job is to overcome the curse of knowledge; classic style is one person showing another person what the writer has seen.

The four agree on more than they disagree. The disagreements (Pinker's defence of certain "rule violations" Strunk would correct, for instance) are about specific judgement calls, not about what good prose is for.
